What Drives the Cost Up (and Down)

A few factors that affect pricing across the board:

  • Number of systems involved. Automating a single tool is cheaper than connecting five tools together. The more integrations, the more complexity.
  • Data quality. If your data is clean and organized, implementation is faster. If it's scattered across spreadsheets, inboxes, and sticky notes, there's cleanup work before automation can start.
  • Compliance requirements. Healthcare (HIPAA), financial services, and government-adjacent businesses require additional security and compliance layers that add 20-50% to costs.
  • Custom vs. off-the-shelf. Some automations use existing AI tools with light customization. Others require custom-built solutions. The audit tells you which is which.
  • Team size and process complexity. More employees means more interviews during the audit and more workflows to map. Larger businesses naturally cost more to assess.

How to Think About ROI

The real question isn't "how much does it cost?" — it's "how much does it return?" Here's the formula we use in every audit:

Annual savings = (hours saved per week) × (hourly rate) × 52 weeks
ROI = annual savings ÷ implementation cost

A $5,000 automation that saves one employee 8 hours per week at $25/hour returns $10,400 per year — a 208% ROI in the first year alone. And that savings compounds every year the automation runs.

Our first client's numbers: $61,600 in annual savings from automations that cost a fraction of that to implement. That's the power of auditing first — you don't guess where the ROI is, you calculate it.
